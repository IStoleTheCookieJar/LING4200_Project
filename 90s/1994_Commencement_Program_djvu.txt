Academic  Customs  and  Dress 

The  origins  of  many  customs  of  the  academic  world  are  to  be  traced  to  the 
Middle  Ages,  particularly  the  eleventh  and  twelfth  centuries  when  the  first 
European  universities  —  Bologna,  Paris  and  Oxford — were  founded. 

In  their  original  organization,  the  universities  were  like  medieval 
corporations  or  guilds.  In  fact  the  word  “universitas”  originally  referred  to 
any  kind  of  corporation.  The  students  at  the  universities  were  equated  to 
guild  apprentices,  and  so  the  completion  of  this  apprenticeship  was 
celebrated  by  a  “commencement”  marking  the  beginning  of  their  careers. 

In  the  twelfth  century,  everyone  wore  gowns.  The  social  and  economic 
status  of  an  individual  was  shown  by  the  extra  features,  such  as  fur 
trimming,  on  the  gowns.  Cloaks  with  hoods  were  worn  over  gowns  for 
protection  in  bad  weather.  Degrees  began  to  be  awarded  in  the  thirteenth 
century,  and  different  styles  of  gowns  and  different  colors  came  to 
represent  the  various  degrees  and  disciplines. 

Over  the  centuries  the  styles  of  dress  changed,  and  by  the  seventeenth 
century,  the  wearingofgownsby  men  had  virtually  ceased.  Buttheuniversities, 
particularly  those  in  Britain,  continued  to  be  conservative  and  strict  in  the 
matter  of  academic  customs  and  dress.  At  Oxford,  for  instance,  students 
could  be  fined  for  cutting  class  or  not  taking  notes,  and  candidates  for 
master’s  degrees  had  to  swear  that  they  owned  a  gown  and  would  always 
wear  it  at  the  proper  times! 

The  first  American  universities  were  founded  on  English  models. 
However,  the  wearing  of  academic  dress  was  not  universal  and  ceased 
during  the  Civil  War.  The  custom  was  revived  in  the  late  nineteenth 
century,  and  in  1895  an  academic  costume  code  was  adopted  which,  with 
modifications,  is  still  used. 

Most  American  universities  have  the  standard  black  gown  with  the 
hood  serving  to  distinguish  the  individual  institutions.  Some  American 
and  European  universities  have  adopted  or  preserved  more  distinctive 
gowns.  There  are  three  styles  of  gowns,  distinguished  mainly  by  the 
sleeves:  the  bachelor's  gown,  with  long,  pointed  sleeves;  the  master’s,  with 
a  squared  sleeve,  cut  so  the  forearm  is  free;  the  doctor’s,  with  bell-shaped 
sleeves  with  three  velvet  bands. 

The  hood  is  the  really  distinguishing  feature  of  modern  gowns, 
indicating  the  degree  (the  width  of  the  edging  band),  the  discipline  (the 
color  of  the  band)  and  the  college  or  university  (the  colors  of  the  lining) . 
Today’s graduates  have  hoods  edged  in  white  (JJA,  MAL.S.),gold  (B.S., 
B.L.S.)  or  drab  (M.BA).  Many  faculty  members  will  have  blue  edging 
ind  icating  the  Ph.D .  degree.  The  faculty  will  be  wearing  hoods  representing 
many  distinguished  institutions  of  higher  education,  e.g.,  navy  blue  and 
orange  for  the  University  of  Virginia,  orange  and  maroon  for  Virginia 
Polytechnic  Institute  and  State  University,  cardinal  and  blue  for  the 
University  of  Pennsylvania.

Academic  Honors  and  Special  Awards 

Academic  achievement  is  recognized  at  Commencement  through  a  variety 
of  honors  and  awards.  Other  awards  were  presented  at  the  Convocation 
ceremony  held  on  Friday,  May  6.  In  addition,  most  academic  departments 
sponsor  chapters  of  honor  societies  which  recognize  student  achievement  in 
specific  disciplines. 

Academic  Distinction 

The  college  recognizes  the  academic  achievement  of  graduating  seniors 
earning  the  degree  of  Bachelor  of  Arts  or  Bachelor  of  Science  through  the 
awards  ofSumma  Cum  Laude,  Magna  Cum  Laude,  and  Cum  Laude,  based 
solely  on  the  student’s  grade  point  average  at  Mary  Washington  College. 
To  graduate  with  Academic  Distinction  a  student  must  earn  at  least  60 
Mary  Washington  College  credits  on  which  grade  points  can  be  com¬ 
puted.  The  cumulative  grade  point  average  to  be  attained  for  each  award

Final  Honors 

Final  Honors  are  awarded  at  Commencement  to  students  who  achieved 
a  grade  point  average  of  3.75  or  higher  in  their  last  56  credits  earned  at 
Mary  Washington  College,  at  least  44  of  which  must  be  graded  credits. 

Departmental  Honors 

Students  may  earn  graduation  with  honors  in  their  major  subjects.  Each 
department  establishes  its  own  requirements  for  honors  projects  or 
theses.  All  departments  require  that  a  student  intending  to  undertake  an 
honors  project  have  a  specified  minimum  grade  point  average  in  the  major 
subject  and  a  minimum  grade  point  average  for  all  work  taken  at  Mary 
Washington  College.

Mortar  Board.  Mortar  Board  is  the  national  senior  honorary  recogniz¬ 
ing  those  students  who  combine  outstanding  records  of  scholarship,

grade  point  average.  To  be  eligible  for  the  Darden  Award,  a  student  must 
have  earned  at  least  94  hours  on  which  quality  points  may  be  computed.

Faculty  Emeriti 

Faculty  members  who  have  served  Mary  Washington  College  for  at 
least  15  years  and  who  have  attained  the  rank  of  professor  or  associate 
professor  are  eligible  upon  retirement  to  be  considered  for  emeritus 
status.  This  honor,  by  action  of  the  Board  of  Visitors,  is  bestowed  this  year 
upon  three  persons. 


Janet  Fraser  Wishner 
Professor  of  Philosophy 

Janet  F.  Wishner  came  to  the  College  25  years  ago  as  assistant 
professor  of  philosophy  —  just  five  years  after  completing  her  under¬ 
graduate  work  at  the  University  of  Leeds  and  earning  a  master’s  degree 
at  Bedford  College,  University  of  London.  She  became  an  associate 
professor  in  1981,  after  the  completion  of  her  doctorate  at  the  University 
of  Georgia.  Dr.  Wishner  has  been  a  professor  of  philosophy  since  1989, 
and  for  four  years,  was  chair  of  the  Department  of  Classics,  Philosophy 
and  Religion.  She  has  served  with  distinction  on  numerous  committees, 
and  has  worked  with  the  B.L.S.  and  MAL.S.  Programs  and  the  Honor 
Council. 


A.  Ray  Merchent 

Executive  Vice  President;  Professor  of  Education 
A  Ray  Merchent  retired  from  Mary  Washington  College  in  November 
1993  after  34  years.  He  arrived  at  the  College  in  1959  as  registrar  and 
instructor  of  education  and  moved  steadily  upwards  to  department  chair, 
to  director  of  admissions,  to  vice  president  for  continuing  education  and 
to  executive  vice  president,  a  post  he  held  from  1980  until  his  retirement. 
He  has  served  on  many  important  College  committees,  including  the 
search  committees  for  two  academic  deans,  and  chaired  the  committee 
that  recommended  Dr.  William  M.  Anderson,  Jr.  for  president  His 
exemplary  service  was  recognized  in  1988  when  he  received  the  College’s 
Washington  Medal  and  in  June  of  1993  when  he  was  made  an  honorary 
alumnus  of  the  College. 


In  Memoriam 
Susan  J.  Hanna 

Distinguished  Professor  of  English 
SusanJ.  Hanna  was  a  member  of  the  Mary  Washington  College  faculty 
from  1968  until  her  untimely  death  in  April  1994.  During  her  26-year 
career  she  achieved  distinction,  not  only  in  the  classroom  as  a  consum¬ 
mate  teacher  of  English,  but  also  in  a  variety  of  faculty  leadership 
positions,  including  chair  of  the  Department  of  English,  Linguistics  and 
Speech.  By  nature  ebullient  she  won  the  admiration  of  students  and 
colleagues  alike  by  virtue  of  her  palpable  love  of  learning  and  her  manifest 


disdain  for  pretentiousness.  As  an  ardent  and  effective  advocate  for  those 
issues  in  which  she  so  passionately  believed.  Dr.  Hanna  also  contributed 
significantly  to  the  welfare  of  the  larger  community  beyond  the  campus. 
Her  worthy  deeds,  both  of  scholarship  and  humanity,  will  long  remain  in 
the  memory  of  all  those  whose  lives  she  touched  throughout  the  Mary 
Washington  community. 


Commencement  Speaker 

Dr.  Arnold  R.  Oliver 

Dr.  Arnold  R.  Oliver  assumed  the  chancellorship  of  the  Virginia 
Community  College  System  on  January  1,  1992.  Prior  to  going  to  Rich¬ 
mond,  Dr.  Oliver  served  as  president  of  Danville  Community  College  for 
five  years,  having  been  the  dean  of  instruction  and  student  development 
at  that  institution  from  1980-86.  He  began  hiscareer  with  the  VCCSin  1972 
as  an  assistant  division  chair  of  the  Humanities  Division  at  Northern 
Virginia  Community  College’s  Annandale  Campus,  moving  on  in  1974  to 
serve  as  a  division  chair  in  Communications  and  Human  Studies  at  the 
Manassas  Campus.  He  served  as  acting  provost  at  NVCC’s  Manassas 
Campus  in  1978-79. 

Dr.  Oliver  has  served  on  a  number  of  VCCS  task  forces  on  subjects 
including  strategic  planning,  resource  distribution,  semester  conversion 
and  curriculum  review,  and  was  appointed  to  the  Virginia  Economic 
Bridge  Advisory  Board.  Dr.  Oliver  has  also  been  active  in  many  profes¬ 
sional  organizations  and  community  groups. 

He  received  a  doctorate  in  higher  education  from  Southern  Illinois 
University  in  1972,  a  master’s  degree  in  English  from  the  University  of 
Idaho  in  1969,  and  a  bachelor’s  degree  from  St  Michael’s  College  in 
Vermont  in  1967. 

Dr.  Oliver  and  his  wife,  Pat,  have  two  children.  They  are  living  in  the 
restored  section  of  Church  Hill  in  Richmond. 


The  Denny  &  Dunipace  Pipe  Band

Denny  &  Dunipace  Pipe  Band  has  been  one  of  America’s  highest  ranking 
championship  bands  for  the  past  20  years.  It  was  formed  in  1970  by 
members  of  the  United  States  Air  Force  Pipe  Band  in  an  effort  to 
encourage  young  pipers  and  drummers  in  the  Washington,  D.C.,  area  to 
discover  and  nourish  their  Celtic  musical  roots.
